Psychological Architecture of Power in Dress

Looking at these fifteen figures, it's clear that fashion is not only used for aesthetic purposes. It also plays a role as a strategic tool for psychological authority. Louis XIV's red heels artificially increased his physical height. However, actual height became a symbolic form of superiority. Queen Elizabeth I's pearl necklace, structured collar, and wide shoulders can similarly represent a presence of authority and power. Napoleon's coronation robe, military uniform and Alexander the Great's armored style represented their power. Catherine de Medici's corset dress seems to reflect a sense of control over her body and the management of her impulses. Cleopatra and Empress Theodora used gold and decoration to turn sacred looks into a sign of power and holiness. In ancient Rome, people wore crowns made of laurel leaves to show they had victory. When we look at Julius Caesar, the crown he wore clearly shows that he hold authority. Queen Victoria's black mourning dress, worn after she became a widow, demonstrated her devotion to her husband and thus conveyed a moral superiority. Marie Antoinette appears to have used excessive ornamentation as an aristocratic exhibit. At the same time, Coco Chanel proves that true independence can be demonstrated in simplicity. Thatcher appears to have used suit style to represent authority, which is coded as masculine. Collectively, these examples show us that fashion has psychological functions related to visibility, hierarchy, gender, and power.
